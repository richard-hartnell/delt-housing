In theory, prices for any good stabilize at that "equiilibrium" point. If supply goes up for some reason, demand is reduced and prices fall. If prices go up for some reason, demand will generally fall.

So if someone -- or someones -- reduced the amount of affordable housing by sequestering it investment portfolios, what would happen to the price?

Middle-men create supply problems.

Landlord-free housing cooperatives make rentals available at around 1/2 of "fair" market rate.


Vienna bought a ton of housing back from local and foreign rent speculators and successfully reduced market-rate rents by ~50%.

What if, instead of pocketing the money and running, we paid tenants back for paying off a home loan?

What if the rent you paid went toward maintaining permanently affordable housing, even while staying affordable while you lived in it?

One possible fix (the short version)

The "dissolving equity land trust" emerges as a unique solution in this environment.


1. Someone makes a down payment on a home (a group of tenants, a nonprofit, a city, etc).

2. Tenants pay off the home loan as part of their rent, like normal. Unlike normal, they are promised a rebate for the portion of their rent that goes toward paying off the home.

3. Once the home is paid off, future tenants pay back the original tenants out of their rent -- and receive their own rebates, albeit at a slightly reduced rate.

4. This process loops until the rebate shrinks to zero.

5. Rent now only slowly escalates close to the costs of homeownership, creating permanently affordable rental housing.
