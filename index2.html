Permanent, affordable rental housing

The quest for sustainable, affordable housing solutions has never been more urgent in the face of escalating housing crises worldwide. A significant and increasing population of workers cannot afford homeownership (or, in many places, even a decent rental), necessitating innovative approaches to address the dire need for accessible housing. Rampant rent speculation has driven prices so high that both temporary rental housing and permanent homes for purchase are increasingly out of reach. The Dissolving Equity Land Trust (DELT) emerges as a groundbreaking model in this landscape, offering a cooperative housing framework that not only promises affordability but also fosters community stability and long-term security for tenants.

Wages have been frozen relative to productivity since the Reagan years. Notably, ballooning rents are included in GDP even though nothing is produced by scalping a house.

FEATURES OF A CO-OP

Conversely, removing rent speculation from rental housing has a number of really nice features.

  • Utilities included in already-low costs
  • Rent escalates slowly, not quickly
  • Faster, cheaper repairs
  • Progressive, not regressive, rents
  • No surprise evictions
  • Keeps money in the community

However, there is one major drawback to the cooperative model: the question of equity. While cooperatives are able to offer housing at dramatically reduced prices, oftimes as little as 1/2 of prevailing market rates, they often require expensive buy-ins. This puts cooperative housing out of reach for working-class tenants who need affordable housing the most.

The DELT solves this last remaining difficulty with cooperative rental housing by dissolving equity payments among far more tenants than usual.

HOW DOES IT WORK?

The DELT has a three-phase life cycle that reduces effective rents to roughly one-half market rate for tenants, no matter when they move in.

The initial phase

At the inception of a DELT, properties are purchased by a trust, which then leases these properties to tenants. Like traditional rental agreements, a portion of the monthly rent paid by tenants goes toward a home loan.

However, unlike traditional rentals, tenants are issued a future rebate for the portion of their rent that pays off home equity.

The dissolving phase

Once the purchase price of the home is paid off, the cost of living is reduced dramatically. However, the DELT keeps rents where they are and allocates extra money toward paying back previous tenants.

Dissolving-phase tenants are also paid a rebate in exchange for paying back the initial-phase tenants. However, in order to avoid creating something like a pyramid scheme with explosive costs, the rebate shrinks every year according to a dynamic mathematical model.

The end phase

Eventually, the home is paid off and the rebate given for equity payments shrinks to zero. The last of the tenant debt is paid off by end-phase tenants and the DELT is now financially solvent, holding home equity in trust.

Although end-phase tenants do not receive a rebate for their rent payments, rent in a mature DELT hovers around one-half of market rate (or possibly less).
